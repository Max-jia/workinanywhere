The first time I needed a doctor abroad, I was in Da Nang, Vietnam. It was nothing dramatic — a sinus infection that had lingered for two weeks and was getting worse, not better. But I was in a country where I spoke about fifteen words of Vietnamese, had no idea which hospitals treated foreigners, and was not sure whether my insurance would cover whatever happened next.

That visit turned out fine. The hospital had English-speaking staff. The bill was $45. My insurance reimbursed me in four days. But the hour I spent Googling "English-speaking doctor Da Nang" at 10pm, feverish and anxious, taught me something: you need to know how this works before you need it.

---

## Telemedicine Changed Everything in 2026

The single biggest improvement for nomad healthcare in the past two years is telemedicine. Most international health insurers now include 24/7 virtual doctor access as standard.

AXA Global Healthcare uses Teladoc Health for virtual consultations — book a phone or video appointment, get a callback within 24 hours, speak to a doctor in your language. Now Health International has "My Daily Telehealth" with chat and video access in six languages including English, Spanish, and Mandarin. These services are included in your plan, not billed separately.

For non-emergency situations — infections, rashes, medication refills, mental health check-ins — a virtual appointment is faster, cheaper, and available in your language. The doctor can prescribe medication and send the prescription to a local pharmacy. You do not need to navigate a foreign hospital system for something your primary care doctor would have handled in ten minutes back home.

## When You Actually Need to Go In Person

For anything that requires a physical examination or immediate treatment, you need a local doctor. Here is how the process works with most international health insurance plans:

1. Find a provider. Most insurer apps (AXA, UHC Global, Cigna) have "Find Care" features with geolocators that show nearby hospitals and clinics. Apps like Doctolib work across Europe.
2. Book an appointment. Tell them you are "self-paying" or "privately insured." This is standard for international insurance — you are not in their national health system.
3. Pay upfront. Most international plans operate on a reimbursement model. You pay the hospital, then submit a claim.
4. Collect documentation: the doctor's report with diagnosis, an itemized bill, and official receipts.
5. Submit the claim through your insurer's mobile app. AXA reports that over 80% of eligible claims are reimbursed within 48 hours.

For large bills — surgeries, hospital stays — request pre-authorization from your insurer. They can often arrange direct billing, where the insurer pays the hospital directly and you never handle the money.

## The Insurance You Actually Need

Travel insurance covers emergencies and repatriation. It does not cover routine checkups, ongoing prescriptions, or chronic condition management. If you are living abroad rather than vacationing, travel insurance is not enough.

International Private Medical Insurance (IPMI) covers both emergencies and routine care. The major providers in 2026 are AXA (five plan levels from Foundation to Prestige Plus), Cigna Global (Silver through Platinum), Now Health International, Allianz Care, Bupa Global, Aetna International, and IMG Global.

Plans vary significantly. Look for: telemedicine included as standard, portability (can you change geographic coverage without re-underwriting?), multilingual support, and digital claims submission through a mobile app.

A plan that requires you to mail paper forms to a PO box in another country is not a plan designed for nomads. Choose one with a functioning app.

## What to Do Before You Get Sick

Identify the nearest English-speaking hospital in every city you visit. Write down the address. Save it offline. Do this on day one, not when you have a fever and can barely read your phone screen.

Carry a basic medical kit: painkillers, antihistamines, antidiarrheal medication, bandages, antiseptic cream. These are the things you need at 2am, not things you want to hunt for in an unfamiliar pharmacy.

Know your insurance policy number and have it saved offline. Know whether your plan requires pre-authorization for certain treatments. Know the 24-hour emergency number. These three pieces of information take five minutes to save and can save you hours of stress when you actually need them.

## FAQ

**Can I use my home country health insurance abroad?**
Almost never for routine care. US health insurance, including Medicare, does not cover treatment outside the US. UK NHS coverage ends when you leave the UK for more than six months. Get a dedicated [international health plan](/blog/travel-insurance-digital-nomads-2026) before you leave.

**What if I have a pre-existing condition?**
Most travel insurance plans exclude pre-existing conditions. International health insurance plans like Genki cover them after a waiting period of 8 to 24 months depending on the condition. Do not lie on your application. If your claim is investigated and the insurer discovers an undisclosed condition, the claim will be denied and your policy may be canceled.

**How do I find an English-speaking doctor right now?**
Open your insurer's app and use the "Find Care" or provider search feature. Alternatively, Google "[city name] international hospital" and look for hospitals with JCI accreditation, which requires English-speaking staff. Most private hospitals in major cities have English-speaking doctors.
