Two weeks into my first stay in Chiang Mai, I got a sinus infection. It started as a cold. Then it became a cough. Then I was running a fever at 2am, googling "English-speaking hospital Chiang Mai" on my phone, uncertain whether my insurance would cover whatever happened next.

A friend told me to go to Bangkok Hospital Chiang Mai. I took a Grab. The receptionist spoke English. A nurse took my vitals within ten minutes. A doctor saw me within twenty. She prescribed antibiotics, a decongestant, and rest. The total bill was about 1,600 baht. $45.

I paid with my credit card. I filed an insurance claim on the AXA app that night. The money was back in my account within four days.

Here is what I learned.

---

## The Thai Healthcare System Is Excellent

Thailand has a two-tier system. Public hospitals serve Thai citizens through the universal healthcare scheme. Private hospitals serve Thais with private insurance and foreign patients. As a digital nomad, you will use the private tier.

The quality of care at private hospitals in major cities is genuinely world-class. Doctors often trained in the US or Europe. Nurses speak English. Facilities are modern. Wait times are short. The cost is a fraction of what you would pay in the US for equivalent care.

Bangkok Hospital, Bumrungrad, and Samitivej are the major private chains. They have branches in Bangkok, Chiang Mai, Phuket, and other cities with significant expat populations. If you are in a rural area without a private hospital nearby, the quality drops. Plan around that.

---

## What Things Cost

A GP consultation runs 500 to 1,500 baht. $14 to $42. Basic lab work runs a few hundred baht. An X-ray costs 1,000 to 3,000 baht. A dental cleaning costs 800 to 1,500 baht. A root canal costs 8,000 to 15,000 baht, compared to $1,500 in the US. The cost savings on dental work alone can justify your insurance premium for the year.

If you need a specialist, the referral process is fast. You see a GP, they refer you, and you see the specialist the same day or the next. There is no six-week wait for a dermatologist appointment. You walk in, you get seen.

---

## What Insurance You Need

Travel insurance from SafetyWing or World Nomads covers emergencies and acute conditions. It will cover the sinus infection I had. It will not cover a routine checkup, an ongoing prescription, or a chronic condition. If you are healthy and under 40, travel insurance is fine for Thailand.

If you have ongoing health needs, you need International Private Medical Insurance. AXA, Cigna Global, and Now Health all cover Thailand. These plans cost more. They also cover routine care, prescriptions, and specialist visits. Get IPMI if you are staying in Thailand long-term or have health conditions you manage regularly.

Thailand requires proof of insurance for some long-stay visas. The DTV visa specifies a coverage minimum. Check your visa requirements before buying a policy.

---

## What to Do Before You Get Sick

Identify the nearest English-speaking private hospital in your city. Save the address offline on your phone. Write down the emergency number. Do not wait until you have a fever at 2am to start searching.

Carry a basic medical kit. Paracetamol, ibuprofen, antihistamines, antidiarrheal medication, bandages, antiseptic cream. These are the things you need at 11pm, not things you want to hunt for at a 7-Eleven that sells mostly snacks and phone chargers.

Know your insurance policy number. Have the 24-hour assistance number saved. Know whether your plan requires pre-authorization for hospital visits. These three things take five minutes to set up and can save you hours of stress when you actually need them.
