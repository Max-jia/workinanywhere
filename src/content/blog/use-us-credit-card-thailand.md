Yes, your US credit card works in Thailand. Visa and Mastercard are accepted at hotels, malls, supermarkets, and larger restaurants. You will still need cash for street food, songthaews, markets, and the motorbike taxi that takes you home at 2am. But your card works.

The question is not whether it works. It is whether it works without costing you money or getting frozen on day two. Three things to do before you land.

---

## 1. Get a Card Without Foreign Transaction Fees

Most standard US credit cards add a 3% foreign transaction fee to every purchase abroad. Your $4 pad thai becomes $4.12. Your $25 grocery run becomes $25.75. Your $600 monthly apartment deposit becomes $618. Over a month of normal spending, this adds up to $40-80 in fees you never see because they are buried in the transaction total.

Cards without this fee include the Capital One Venture X, Chase Sapphire Preferred, and Wells Fargo Autograph. The Autograph has no annual fee and earns 3x points on travel and dining. If you only make one change before going to Thailand, make it this one.

---

## 2. Tell Your Bank You Are Going, Then Prepare for Them to Ignore You

I told Chase my travel dates and destination three days before flying to Bangkok. My card still got frozen on day two because the fraud algorithm flagged a 7-Eleven purchase at 10pm local time.

Most banks let you set a travel notice through their app or website. Do it. Then bring a backup card from a different bank on a different network. If your Chase Visa gets frozen, your Capital One Mastercard will still work. Store them in different places. If your wallet gets stolen, you do not want both cards in it.

---

## 3. Always Pay in Baht

Every card terminal in Thailand will ask: "Pay in USD or THB?" Pick THB. Every single time. No exceptions.

If you pick USD, the merchant's bank converts the amount at whatever rate they choose, usually 3-6% worse than the Visa or Mastercard network rate. Your no-foreign-transaction-fee card becomes meaningless because the fee is baked into the exchange rate instead of appearing as a line item.

This is called dynamic currency conversion. It is legal. It is predatory. And you defeat it by always, always choosing the local currency.

---

## What Works and What Does Not

Contactless payments are everywhere in Thailand now. 7-Eleven, Tops, Big C, Central malls all accept Apple Pay and Google Wallet. You can leave your physical card at home and pay with your phone in most places.

But street food stalls, night markets, songthaews, and smaller shops are cash only. Withdraw baht from an ATM inside a bank branch during business hours. If the machine eats your card, you walk inside and talk to someone. A standalone ATM on a street corner at midnight does not offer this option.

Do not use a debit card for everyday purchases. If your debit card gets skimmed or compromised, the thief has direct access to your checking account. Credit cards have better fraud protection. Use credit for purchases, debit only for ATM withdrawals, and keep your debit card in your apartment safe when you go out.
