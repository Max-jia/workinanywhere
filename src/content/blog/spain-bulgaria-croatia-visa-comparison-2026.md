If you have a remote job and want to live in Europe, the digital nomad visa is your easiest legal path. Three EU countries dominate the conversation right now: Spain, Bulgaria, and Croatia. Searches for all three are spiking. Google Trends shows "spain's digital nomad visa" up 140% in the past week and "bulgaria digital nomad visa" up 40% in the past month.

The problem is most comparison articles list numbers without telling you what those numbers mean in practice. An income threshold of €2,849 looks similar to €2,870 on a spreadsheet. In real life, one of those gets you three years in Barcelona with a massive tax break, and the other gets you one year in Split with zero tax but no easy way to stay longer.

I have written separate deep-dive guides on each country. This article is the direct comparison for someone trying to pick one.

## The Numbers at a Glance

| What Matters | Spain | Bulgaria | Croatia |
|-------------|-------|----------|---------|
| Monthly income required | €2,849 | ~€950 (or savings) | ~€2,870 |
| Initial permit | 3 years | 1 year | 1 year |
| Renewable | Yes, 2 more years | Yes, 1 year | Only after 6 months out |
| Tax on foreign income | 24% flat (Beckham Law) | 10% flat | 0% |
| Path to permanent residency | Yes, after 5 years | Yes, after 5 years | No direct path |
| Schengen access | Yes | Yes (since 2026) | Yes (since 2023) |
| Application fee | ~€80 | €300+ | ~€50-100 |
| Processing time | 20 days (in-country) | 30-60 days | ~30 days |
| Monthly living cost (single) | €1,800-2,800 | €800-1,300 | €1,200-1,800 |

Sources: Spain's BOE Royal Decree 126/2026; Bulgaria's Ministry of Interior; Croatia's Ministry of Interior; Numbeo cost data, July 2026.

## Spain: The Long-Term Play

Spain gives you the longest initial permit of the three and the clearest path to staying. Three years upfront means you are not filling out renewal paperwork twelve months after you unpack. The Beckham Law tax regime, which I covered in detail in my Spain visa guide, lets you pay 24% flat on Spanish-source income and exempts most foreign income from Spanish taxation.

The income threshold is high: €2,849 per month for 2026, pegged to 200% of Spain's minimum wage. That number goes up every January when the SMI adjusts. If you are earning comfortably above that floor and want to build a life in Europe, Spain is the best option of the three.

The downside is cost of living. Barcelona and Madrid rent is expensive by European standards. A one-bedroom in a central Barcelona neighborhood runs €1,200-1,800. You can bring that down by living in Valencia, Seville, or Granada, but the cost gap between Spain and Bulgaria is roughly 2x across the board.

### Who Spain is best for

- You earn enough to comfortably clear the €2,849 threshold
- You want to stay in Europe long-term and potentially get permanent residency
- You are willing to exchange higher costs for better infrastructure and a larger nomad community
- You want the Beckham Law tax benefit (worth it if you earn over €50,000/year)

## Bulgaria: The Budget EU Option

Bulgaria is the cheapest capital city in the EU, and its digital nomad visa has the lowest income requirement in the bloc. At roughly €950 per month, the threshold is accessible to freelancers and remote workers who would not qualify for Spain or Croatia.

The tax situation is also excellent. Bulgaria has a flat 10% personal income tax rate, the lowest in the EU. Combined with low living costs (a comfortable solo life in Sofia runs €800-1,300 per month), Bulgaria lets you keep more of what you earn.

The catch is that Bulgaria is not as developed as Spain. Sofia has coworking spaces, fast internet, and a growing expat community, but the infrastructure is rougher, the winters are cold and gray, and administrative processes are slower. The visa application fee is also higher (€300+) despite the lower cost of living. Bulgarian government websites and forms are primarily in Bulgarian. You will need help from a local lawyer or translator to navigate the process.

One important caveat: some 2026 sources report that Bulgaria's income threshold may have been revised upward to roughly €2,583 per month (€31,000 per year) under recent amendments. The lower €950 figure comes from the older freelance-permit pathway. Before applying, verify the current requirement with Bulgaria's Migration Directorate or a Bulgarian immigration lawyer. The rules have been in flux since Bulgaria joined Schengen.

### Who Bulgaria is best for

- You want the lowest possible cost of living in the EU
- Your income is solid but below the Spain threshold
- You are fine with a smaller expat community and more DIY bureaucracy
- You want the 10% flat tax rate and do not need the Beckham Law's foreign-income exemption

## Croatia: The Tax-Free Coastline

Croatia has one thing that neither Spain nor Bulgaria can match: zero tax on foreign income during the visa period. If you earn your money from outside Croatia, the Croatian tax authority does not touch it.

The trade-off is that the visa is a one-year arrangement with no clear renewal path. You can stay for a year, but after that you must leave for at least six months before reapplying. There is no direct path to permanent residency through the digital nomad visa. This makes Croatia a great option for a one-year base, but a poor choice if your goal is EU long-term residency.

The income threshold sits between Spain and Bulgaria at roughly €2,870 per month. Living costs in Split or Zagreb (€1,200-1,800 per month) are lower than Barcelona but higher than Sofia.

One detail I do not see mentioned often: Croatia joined Schengen in 2023, which means your year in Croatia counts as Schengen time. If you are not an EU citizen, this matters for the 90/180-day Schengen rule. Spending a year in Croatia on this visa does not eat into your Schengen tourist allowance because the visa itself grants you the right to be there.

### Who Croatia is best for

- You want a one-year European base with zero tax on foreign income
- You do not need a path to permanent residency
- You value coastal lifestyle and Mediterranean climate
- You earn enough to clear the ~€2,870 threshold but want to keep more of your income

## The Decision Framework

Ask yourself three questions. The answers will tell you which visa to pursue.

**1. How long do you want to stay in Europe?**

If the answer is "as long as possible, maybe permanently," pick Spain. The three-year initial permit and five-year path to permanent residency are unmatched. Bulgaria offers a path to permanent residency after five years as well, but the shorter initial permit means more renewal paperwork along the way. Croatia is the wrong choice for long-term plans.

**2. How much do you earn?**

If you earn less than €2,000 per month, Bulgaria is your only realistic option of the three. Between €2,000 and €2,800, Bulgaria is still the easiest threshold to meet, and Croatia sits at the upper edge. Above €2,800, all three are open to you, and the decision shifts to taxes and lifestyle.

**3. Do you care more about saving on taxes or saving on living costs?**

If taxes are your biggest expense, Croatia (0% on foreign income) wins, followed by Bulgaria (10% flat), then Spain (24% with Beckham Law). If living costs are your biggest expense, Bulgaria wins by a wide margin, Sofia at €800-1,300 per month is roughly half of Barcelona.

## What I Would Do

If I had to pick one for myself right now: Spain.

Not because it is the cheapest (it is not) or the most tax-advantaged (it is not). Because it is the one where you can actually build a life. Three years of stability, a path to permanent residency, the largest nomad community in Europe, and a tax deal that, while not zero, is dramatically better than standard Spanish rates.

If my income did not meet the Spain threshold, I would go to Bulgaria and spend the first year finding a way to increase my income, then reassess. Croatia I would treat as a one-year sabbatical destination. Beautiful year, low tax, move on.
