The goal of learning a language when you move abroad isn't fluency. It's functionality — being able to order food, ask for directions, handle basic transactions, and show respect by trying.

You don't need to be perfect. You need to be able to live your life. Here's a practical, three-phase approach for getting there fast.

---

## Phase 1: Foundation (4–6 Weeks Before Moving)

Before you arrive, build a base of 500–1,000 core words and basic phrases. Spend 15–30 minutes a day.

### Best Apps for This Phase

| App | Best For | Price |
|-----|----------|-------|
| **Babbel** | Structured grammar + real-life dialogues (ordering food, small talk) | ~$8–9/month |
| **Pimsleur** | Audio-first, speaking from day one — best for pronunciation confidence | ~$20/month per language |
| **Memrise** | Vocabulary firepower — spaced repetition with real native speaker videos | ~$5/month |
| **Duolingo** | Daily habit-building — good as a supplement, not your primary tool | Free / $7/month Super |

**What this phase gets you:** You can say hello, thank you, excuse me, order food, ask for the bathroom, count, and understand basic directions. That's enough to survive your first week.

---

## Phase 2: Active Immersion (First 1–3 Months After Arrival)

Once you're in the country, shift from studying to using.

### Daily Habits That Move the Needle

- **Switch your phone to the target language.** You already know where every button is. You'll absorb vocabulary passively.
- **Consume local media.** Netflix with target-language subtitles. Local news radio in the background. Music with lyrics you look up.
- **Use the language for real tasks.** Order at restaurants in the local language. Do your grocery shopping in it. Ask for directions even when you know where you're going. Every real interaction is practice.
- **Learn 5 new words a day from real life.** The word you learned because you needed it at the pharmacy sticks better than the word from a flashcard.

### Best Apps for This Phase

| App | Best For | Price |
|-----|----------|-------|
| **Speak** | AI conversation partner — practice speaking without fear of judgment | ~$10/month |
| **HelloTalk** | Live conversation exchange with native speakers | Free / $7/month |
| **LingQ** | Import real content (podcasts, news, YouTube) and learn from what you actually enjoy | ~$10–12/month |

## Phase 3: Full Immersion (Months 2–6)

### Book a Tutor

Two 30-minute sessions per week with a native speaker on italki ($10–25/hour depending on language) is the single highest-return investment you can make. An app can teach you vocabulary. A person teaches you how people actually speak — the shortcuts, the idioms, the rhythm.

### Speak Badly, Often

The biggest barrier to language learning isn't cognitive — it's emotional. Fear of sounding stupid keeps people silent. The people who learn fastest are the ones who are willing to speak badly, make mistakes, and keep going. Every mistake is a correction you won't need to make again.

### The 80/20 Rule

You don't need to learn everything. Focus on the 20% of vocabulary and grammar that covers 80% of daily interactions: greetings, numbers, food, directions, transactions, basic verbs (to be, to have, to go, to want, to need), past and future tense for those verbs. Everything else is optimization.

---

## What Doesn't Work

- **Relying only on gamified apps.** Duolingo is excellent for building a daily habit, but learners who only use Duolingo typically plateau and can't hold real conversations. It's a supplement, not a primary strategy.
- **Passive-only learning.** If an app doesn't make you speak aloud within the first week, it's optimized for recognition, not production. Recognition and production are different skills.
- **Waiting until you're "ready" to speak.** You will never feel ready. Start speaking badly on day one. It's the only way to get better.
