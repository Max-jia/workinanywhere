I've lived in both. They're the two most recommended mid-budget nomad cities on the planet, and for good reason. Both offer excellent value, thriving communities, and a quality of life that makes your friends back home jealous.

But here's the thing: they're completely different experiences. If you pick the wrong one for your personality, you'll be booking a flight out within a month. Here's what nobody tells you.

---

## The Feel of Each City

Chiang Mai is calm. It's temples and street food and a slower rhythm that makes you wonder why you ever rushed through life. Mornings start with a $1.50 khao soi from a street stall, afternoons are for coworking on Nimmanhaemin Road, evenings are for night markets. The city wraps around you. It's comfortable in the best way.

Medellín is energy. Salsa music from open windows, the buzz of Spanish conversations in every cafe, a skyline framed by green mountains. You feel alive here in a way that's hard to describe — it's louder, more social, more *happening*. If Chiang Mai is a warm blanket, Medellín is a shot of espresso.

Neither is better. But one is probably better *for you*.

## What Your Money Gets You

| | Chiang Mai | Medellín |
|---|---|---|
| Studio/1-bed | $250–400 | $500–750 |
| Street food meal | $1–3 | $3–6 |
| Coworking monthly | $70–150 | $80–200 |
| Monthly total (comfortable) | **$900–1,200** | **$1,200–1,600** |

Chiang Mai wins on pure value. Your money goes further here than almost anywhere else with comparable infrastructure. But "cheaper" isn't the same as "better value." Medellín costs more and delivers more — better weather, easier time zones for North American clients, a more social environment. Whether that premium is worth it depends on what you value.

---

## The Climate (This Is the Real Difference)

This is where these cities diverge most dramatically, and honestly, it's the reason many people choose one over the other.

Medellín has near-perfect weather. 63-83°F year-round. No AC needed. No heating. Open your windows and you're comfortable 365 days a year. They don't call it the "City of Eternal Spring" for nothing.

Chiang Mai is hot. Like, *actually* hot, especially March through June. And then there's burning season — February to April, when agricultural fires blanket northern Thailand in hazardous smog. I cannot overstate how bad this gets. Masks. Air purifiers running 24/7. A lot of experienced nomads leave entirely during these months, and you should plan to do the same.

**The verdict:** If you have seasonal flexibility and don't mind heat, Chiang Mai's value makes it worth planning around burning season. If you want consistent, comfortable weather without having to think about it, Medellín wins by a mile.

---

## Community & Social Life

Chiang Mai has the larger nomad community — about 20,000 remote workers at any given time. It's easy to meet people. The pace is social but relaxed: coworking during the day, night markets and street food crawls in the evening. English is widely spoken in nomad areas.

Medellín has about 11,400 nomads — smaller but more energetic. The nightlife is significantly livelier. If you want to go out dancing on a Tuesday, Medellín delivers. But English is less common outside the expat bubble, and you'll need basic Spanish to feel comfortable in daily life.

**Pick Chiang Mai if** you want a calm, friendly community and an easy social experience. **Pick Medellín if** you want more nightlife, more energy, and are willing to learn some Spanish.

---

## Safety

I need to be honest here: Chiang Mai is one of the safest cities on any nomad list. Violent crime against foreigners is extremely rare. Your biggest risk is a traffic accident on a motorbike.

Medellín is more nuanced. El Poblado and Laureles are safe if you follow basic precautions. Outside established expat zones, the situation changes quickly. Phone snatching and petty theft happen. You need situational awareness — don't flash valuables, don't walk alone late at night in unfamiliar areas, and use Uber rather than hailing taxis on the street. Plenty of nomads live in Medellín for years without incident. Plenty of others have had their phone snatched on day three. Both are true.

## Visas

| | Chiang Mai | Medellín |
|---|---|---|
| Long-term visa | DTV: 5 years, 180-day stays | DN Visa: 2 years |
| Key requirement | 500,000 THB savings (~$14,500) | ~$1,450/month income |
| Income proof needed? | No | Yes (3 months, no averaging) |
| Fee | ~$280–400 | ~$75 |

Medellín's visa has a lower financial barrier and a dedicated nomad framework. Chiang Mai's DTV is more flexible long-term but requires significant liquid savings. If you don't have $14,500 in the bank, Medellín is the more accessible option.

---

## Time Zone (This Matters More Than You Think)

Chiang Mai is UTC+7 — 12 hours ahead of US East Coast. If you have regular meetings with American clients or teammates, you're working nights. Some people thrive on this. Most don't.

Medellín is UTC-5 — same as US Eastern time. Your workday aligns naturally with North American schedules. For anyone working with US or Canadian clients, this alone can tip the decision.

For European clients, the gap is smaller and Chiang Mai's time zone works fine.

---

## Which One Should You Choose?

**Go to Chiang Mai if:**
- You're budget-conscious or just starting out
- You value calm, safety, and community depth
- Food is a major priority (it should be — Chiang Mai is legendary)
- You work with Asia-Pacific or European clients
- You can leave during burning season (Feb–April)

**Go to Medellín if:**
- You work with North American clients and need time zone overlap
- Perfect weather is non-negotiable for you
- You want an energetic, social environment
- You speak or are excited to learn Spanish
- You can budget $1,200+/month

**Or do what I did:** spend 3-4 months in each. Chiang Mai during the good season (May–January), Medellín during burning season (February–April). Follow the weather. Both cities are worth your time.
