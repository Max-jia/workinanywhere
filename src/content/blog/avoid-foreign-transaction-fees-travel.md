I did the math on my first year as a nomad. I spent about $24,000 abroad, mostly on a standard US debit card. The foreign transaction fees, ATM surcharges, and hidden exchange rate markups cost me roughly $720. That is a round-trip flight to Tokyo. I paid it to my bank for the privilege of accessing my own money.

Stop it.

---

## The Three Ways You Are Paying Without Knowing

Your bank takes a cut every time you spend money abroad. It happens three different ways and you only see one of them.

**Foreign transaction fee.** This is the obvious one. A 2-3% surcharge added to every purchase. A $4 coffee in Bangkok becomes $4.12 on your statement. A $1,200 apartment deposit becomes $1,236. Most standard debit and credit cards charge this. Most travel cards do not. If your card description does not explicitly say "no foreign transaction fees," it charges them.

**ATM withdrawal fees.** Three separate charges hit a single ATM withdrawal abroad. Your bank charges you $3-5 for using a non-network ATM. The ATM owner charges you another $3-5. And the exchange rate includes a spread of 1-3%. A single withdrawal of $200 can cost $10-15 in fees. Do this twice a week and you are losing $80-120 a month to ATMs.

**Dynamic currency conversion.** When an ATM or card terminal asks "pay in USD or local currency?" and you pick USD, you just agreed to a 3-6% markup. Always choose local currency. Let your bank do the conversion at the card network rate. Never let the merchant or ATM do it.

---

## What to Set Up Before You Leave

A Wise or Revolut account. Both give you local bank details in multiple currencies, convert at the mid-market rate, and charge a transparent fee of 0.33-0.77% instead of the 2-3% your bank buries in the exchange rate. Wise lets you hold 40 currencies and spend in 150 countries. Revolut adds budgeting tools and virtual cards.

A credit card with no foreign transaction fees. The Capital One Venture X, Chase Sapphire Preferred, and Wells Fargo Autograph all skip the foreign transaction fee. The Autograph has no annual fee. Get one of these and use it for every purchase abroad. Your debit card stays in your apartment safe for ATM withdrawals only.

---

## The One Rule That Saves the Most Money

Always pay in the local currency. The terminal will ask. The ATM will ask. Pick baht in Thailand, pesos in Colombia, euros in Spain. Never pick your home currency. The conversion rate the merchant offers is always worse than what your bank or Wise will give you.

This single rule saved me more money than changing banks did. Every time you pick "USD" on a foreign terminal, you are paying a stranger to convert your money at whatever rate they choose. Do not.
