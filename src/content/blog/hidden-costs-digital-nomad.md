Only 11% of digital nomads say they have more money left over each month compared to their previous life. That number comes from the bunq Global Living Report, which surveyed 7,100 people across eight countries. It is the single most sobering statistic in the nomad economy.

The math looks great on paper. Earn a US salary. Live in Thailand. Bank the difference. But the banked difference keeps getting eaten by costs that do not appear in any budget template. Here is where the money actually goes.

---

## The Costs Nobody Budgets For

**Visa fees and border runs.** Every new country has a visa. The fee — $50, $200, $500 — is just the start. Add the cost of document preparation, notarization, translation, apostille services, and the border run flight you booked because your extension was denied. Budget $100-300/month just for staying legal.

**Equipment failure and replacement.** Your laptop is your livelihood. When it breaks — and it will, eventually — you are buying a replacement immediately, in whatever country you are in, at whatever price they are charging. Add chargers, cables, adapters, power banks, and the external hard drive you bought because you suddenly got paranoid about data loss. Budget $50-100/month.

**Transport within cities.** You will use Grab, Uber, taxis, and scooter rentals more than you expect. In cities without good public transit, transport is your second-biggest expense after rent. A month of daily rides adds up to $150-300 faster than you think.

**Social spending.** This is not optional. You build community by going to coworking drinks, group dinners, weekend trips. If you skip every social event to save money, you will be lonely and you will burn out. Budget $200-400/month for the activities that keep you sane and connected.

**Healthcare beyond insurance.** Even with good insurance, you pay copays and deductibles. Dental emergencies happen. A root canal in Thailand costs $300 instead of $1,500, but $300 is still $300 you were not expecting to spend. Budget $50-150/month for out-of-pocket health costs.

---

## What This Adds Up To

If you are spending $150 on visas, $75 on equipment, $200 on transport, $300 on social activities, and $100 on health — that is $825/month before you have paid for rent, food, or coworking. These are the costs that eat the geo-arbitrage savings.

The nomads I know who are actually saving money have one thing in common: they budget for these costs as fixed expenses, not surprises. They do not pretend equipment will last forever or that they will never go out. They build the real numbers into their monthly plan and adjust their lifestyle around what is left.

---

## The Real Budget Formula

Rent + food + coworking = your visible budget. That is what every nomad budget guide shows you.

Visas + equipment + transport + social + health = your invisible budget. That is what actually determines whether you save money.

Add them together. Budget for the real number. The alternative is being surprised every month by costs that were always going to show up.
