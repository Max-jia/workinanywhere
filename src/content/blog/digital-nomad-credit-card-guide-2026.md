If you earn in one currency and spend in another, your credit card is either saving you money or quietly draining your account. The difference between the right card and the wrong one is roughly 3 to 5 percent of everything you spend. On $2,000 a month in living expenses, that is $720 to $1,200 a year. Just from picking the wrong piece of plastic.

Three things go wrong for digital nomads with credit cards. Your bank charges you a hidden fee on every purchase abroad and buries it in the exchange rate. Your card gets frozen at the worst possible moment because the fraud algorithm does not understand that you actually live in six countries. And you miss out on travel rewards that could be covering your flights.

This guide covers all three problems, plus the cards that fix them.

## The Problem No. 1: Foreign Transaction Fees

Most US credit cards charge a foreign transaction fee of 2.7 to 3 percent on every purchase made outside the United States. This fee is not shown as a separate line item. It is embedded in the exchange rate your bank applies to the transaction.

A $3 coffee in Thailand does not ring up as $3. It rings up as $3.09. Multiply that by every meal, every Grab ride, every 7-Eleven run, every month. That is $60 to $90 a month in fees you never see on a line item.

**The fix:** Use a card with no foreign transaction fees. The best options for US-based nomads:

| **Chase Sapphire Preferred** | $95 | None | 3× points on dining and travel, strong travel protections |
| **Capital One Venture** | $95 | None | 2× miles on everything, simple redemption |
| **Capital One VentureOne** | $0 | None | 1.25× miles, no annual fee |
| **Chase Sapphire Reserve** | $550 | None | 3× points, $300 annual travel credit, airport lounge access |
| **Bilt Mastercard** | $0 | None | Earn points on rent payments with no fee |

If you pay rent abroad and want to earn points on it, Bilt is the only card that allows this without a transaction fee. If you want a card with zero annual fee and zero foreign transaction fees, Capital One VentureOne is the simplest choice.

## The Problem No. 2: Your Card Will Get Frozen

Banks use algorithms to detect fraud. If you normally use your card in San Francisco and suddenly it is buying pad thai in Chiang Mai, the algorithm assumes theft and freezes the card. This happens to nomads constantly, usually at the worst possible moment.

A frozen card abroad is not like a frozen card at home. You cannot walk into a branch. Customer service may not have a toll-free number in your country. If you only brought one card, you are now in a foreign country with no way to pay for anything.

Here is what to do before you leave.

### Set travel notifications

Most banks now claim they do not need travel notices because their fraud detection is "smart enough." Do not believe them. Log into your account anyway and set travel notifications for every card. List every country you plan to visit in the next three months. Update the list when your plans change.

### Bring at least two cards from different banks

If Chase freezes your Sapphire, your Capital One Venture should still work. Different banks, different fraud algorithms, different risk of simultaneous freeze. Also carry a debit card from a third institution as a backup for ATM cash if both credit cards go down.

### Keep your bank's international number somewhere offline

Write down the international collect-call number for each bank. Not the 1-800 number. The number that works from outside the US. Most banks have one. Save it in a note on your phone and on a piece of paper in your wallet. When your card is frozen, you will need it.

### What to do if it happens anyway

Call the number. Verify your identity. Ask them to unfreeze the card and note on your account that you are a long-term traveler, not a one-week tourist. Most banks can add a "frequent traveler" flag that reduces future freezes. If the first agent cannot help, ask for the fraud department directly.

## The Problem No. 3: You Are Leaving Points on the Table

The best travel cards earn 2× to 5× points on categories nomads actually spend in: dining, travel, and everyday purchases. These points translate to flights, hotel nights, and statement credits. A nomad spending $2,000 a month on a travel card can earn 50,000 to 100,000 points per year, enough for a round-trip international flight.

The cards that earn the most for nomads:

- **Chase Sapphire Reserve:** 3× on dining and travel (including Grab, Uber, airlines, hotels, and even some coworking spaces coded as travel). $300 annual travel credit effectively reduces the $550 annual fee to $250. Priority Pass lounge access covers you at airports worldwide. Points transfer to 14 airline and hotel partners.

- **American Express Gold:** 4× on dining and groceries. The $250 annual fee is offset by $120 in dining credits and $120 in Uber credits annually. No foreign transaction fee. The catch: Amex is accepted in fewer places internationally than Visa or Mastercard, especially in Southeast Asia and Latin America.

- **Capital One Venture X:** 2× on everything. $395 annual fee, offset by $300 annual travel credit and 10,000 anniversary points. Capital One lounge access and Priority Pass. The flat 2× rate means you do not have to think about bonus categories. Simple and effective.

## The One Thing Nobody Tells You About Dynamic Currency Conversion

Every time you swipe your card abroad, the terminal or ATM will ask: "Pay in local currency or your home currency?"

Always choose **local currency.** Always. No exceptions.

When you choose your home currency, the merchant's bank sets the exchange rate rather than Visa or Mastercard. That rate is typically 3 to 6 percent worse than the card network rate. This is called Dynamic Currency Conversion, and it is one of the most profitable scams in the payments industry because most travelers do not know it exists.

The card network rate (Visa/Mastercard) is close to the mid-market rate. The DCC rate is not. Choose local currency every single time, and check your receipt to make sure the merchant did not ignore your choice.

## How to Set Up Your Wallet Before You Leave

A nomad-ready wallet has three layers:

1. **Daily spending card** — No foreign transaction fee, earns points on dining and travel. Chase Sapphire Preferred or Capital One Venture.
2. **Backup card** — From a different bank. Also no foreign transaction fee. Even if it earns fewer points, the redundancy is worth it.
3. **ATM debit card** — Charles Schwab Bank reimburses all ATM fees worldwide. Fidelity Cash Management Account does the same. Both refund the $6 ATM fees common in Thailand and elsewhere. Never use a credit card for cash advances. The fees are obscene.

Set up Apple Pay or Google Pay with all three cards as a backup. If your physical wallet is lost or stolen, you can still pay with your phone while you sort out replacements.

## FAQ

**Do I need to tell my bank I am traveling?**
Yes. Even if they say it is not necessary. Set travel notices for every card you carry. List every country you plan to visit. The five minutes this takes prevents hours of phone calls from a foreign country with a frozen card.

**What should I do if my card is frozen abroad?**
Call the international number for your bank. Ask them to unfreeze the card and add a frequent traveler note to your account. If you only brought one card, use a backup payment method (Apple Pay, secondary card, cash) until the issue is resolved.

**Can I use a US credit card in Thailand without being charged fees?**
Yes, if your card has no foreign transaction fee. Most Chase, Capital One, and American Express travel cards waive this fee. Always choose "pay in Thai Baht" when the terminal asks. For more details, [read our guide to using US cards in Thailand](/blog/use-us-credit-card-thailand).
