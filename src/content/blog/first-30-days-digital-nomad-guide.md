The first month as a digital nomad is disorienting. You've left your old structure behind and haven't built a new one yet. Everything feels simultaneously exciting and overwhelming.

Here's a practical timeline for your first 30 days — the things that matter, in the order they matter.

---

## Before You Leave (Week 0)

### Finances
- Set up a Wise or Revolut account for cross-border banking
- Get a credit card with no foreign transaction fees
- Notify your bank you'll be traveling (frozen cards are a preventable crisis)
- Set aside an emergency fund of at least 2 months' expenses

### Insurance
- Buy travel or nomad health insurance before departure
- Save your policy number offline on your phone

### Documents
- Digital copies of passport, visa, insurance, vaccination records on encrypted cloud storage
- Printed passport copies stored separately from your actual passport
- Extra passport photos (some visas require physical copies)

### Packing
- Pack for 7-10 days, not 30. You can do laundry.
- Electronics: laptop, noise-canceling headphones, unlocked phone, universal power adapter, power bank
- Merino wool clothing if you can afford it — odor-resistant, dries fast, wears longer between washes
- If you're not sure whether to bring something, don't. You can buy almost anything locally.

## Day 1–3: Land and Stabilize

Your only jobs in the first three days:

1. **Get a local SIM card or activate an eSIM.** Data is infrastructure. Do this before you leave the airport or immediately after.
2. **Find your accommodation and unpack.** Don't try to be productive on arrival day. Settle in. Find the nearest grocery store, pharmacy, and coffee shop.
3. **Walk your neighborhood.** Orient yourself. What's nearby? Where's the nearest coworking space? Which cafes look laptop-friendly? Walking also helps with jet lag.
4. **Set up your workspace.** Test the WiFi. Find a desk or table that works. If it doesn't, identify the nearest coworking space and buy a day pass for tomorrow.

---

## Day 4–7: Build the Basics

1. **Establish a morning routine.** Keep it simple and location-independent: wake up → water → walk (15–30 min, no phone) → first work session. The specifics can shift, but the sequence stays the same.
2. **Get your first real work done.** Ship something. The anxiety of "can I actually be productive here?" disappears once you deliver.
3. **Find your coworking space.** Try 2–3 spaces with day passes before committing to a monthly membership. The vibe matters more than the amenities.
4. **Join the local nomad groups.** Search "[city] digital nomads" or "[city] expats" on WhatsApp, Telegram, and Facebook. Post: "Just arrived, staying for [X months], working in [field]. Anyone around for coffee?" This is normal. You won't look desperate.
5. **Buy a local SIM if you haven't already.** eSIMs are fine for arrival, but local SIMs are cheaper for longer stays.

---

## Week 2: Go Deeper

1. **Say yes to everything social for one week.** Coworking event that sounds boring? Go. Group dinner with people you just met? Go. The window for integrating into a social circle is short. Front-load your social effort.
2. **Find your "third place."** Not home, not work — a cafe, park, or bar you can return to regularly. Familiarity is grounding when everything else is new.
3. **Explore beyond the tourist zone.** Go to a neighborhood where locals actually live. Eat somewhere without an English menu. This is where you figure out if you actually like a city.
4. **Start tracking your spending.** The first month's expenses are always higher than expected (setup costs, SIM cards, deposits, initial grocery runs). Track everything so you know what your real monthly run rate looks like.

---

## Week 3–4: Lock In

1. **Evaluate your workspace.** Is the coworking space working? Are you productive in your accommodation? Adjust now — don't spend three months in a setup that isn't serving you.
2. **Build a social routine.** Identify 1–2 recurring events (weekly coworking drinks, a language exchange, a sports group) and attend consistently. Familiarity builds connection.
3. **Plan your next move — but don't rush.** If you're enjoying where you are, stay longer. The "slowmad" pace (2–3 months per location) is more sustainable and socially rewarding than moving every 2–3 weeks.
4. **Reflect.** What's working? What's harder than you expected? Adjust your approach for the next destination based on what you've learned.

---

## Common First-Month Mistakes

1. **Moving too fast.** Trying to see 5 cities in 30 days. You'll be exhausted and won't genuinely experience any of them. Pick one place. Stay.
2. **Over-planning activities and under-planning work.** It's tempting to treat your first month like a vacation. It's not. Protect your work hours.
3. **Not joining groups before arriving.** Showing up alone and trying to meet people organically from scratch is the hardest way to do it. Join the groups. Post the message.
4. **Comparing your behind-the-scenes to everyone else's highlight reel.** Instagram shows rooftop pools, not the Tuesday night you spent alone wondering if this was a mistake. Both are real. The first month is hard for almost everyone.

---

**Bottom line:** The first 30 days are about stabilization, not optimization. Land. Get connected. Ship some work. Meet a few people. Everything else can wait for month two.
