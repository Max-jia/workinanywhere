Everyone tells you remote jobs are everywhere. Then you look at the listings and every single one wants three to five years of experience, a degree you do not have, and "proven track record of remote work."

It feels like a door that only opens from the inside.

Companies hired over 400,000 entry level remote workers in the first half of 2026 alone. Not developers. Not senior managers. Customer service reps, virtual assistants, data entry clerks, content moderators. The jobs exist. They are just not the ones LinkedIn puts in your feed.

---

## The jobs that actually hire beginners

Some remote job titles show up over and over for entry level positions. These are the ones to target first.

**Customer service representative.** This is the single biggest entry point. Companies like TTEC, Concentrix, and LiveOps hire constantly. The work is handling calls, chats, or emails from customers. Pay ranges from $15 to $22 an hour. Most of these companies provide training. You need a quiet room, a reliable internet connection, and the ability to stay calm when someone is yelling at you about their missing package.

**Virtual assistant.** A VA handles email, scheduling, data entry, customer support, and whatever else a busy founder does not want to do. The barrier to entry is almost nonexistent. You need to be organized, responsive, and comfortable with basic tools like Google Workspace and Notion. Most VAs start on platforms like Belay, Time Etc, or Upwork and build from there. The hourly range is $18 to $35 depending on specialization.

**Data entry clerk.** Repetitive, unglamorous, and surprisingly steady. Data entry means transferring information between systems, updating records, or processing forms. These jobs rarely require experience beyond basic computer skills. Pay runs $14 to $19 an hour. Companies like Axion Data Services and Clickworker hire in batches.

**Content moderator.** Social media platforms outsource content review to thousands of remote workers. The work can be emotionally taxing, and companies typically require a mental health support plan. But these jobs are genuinely entry level and pay $17 to $22 an hour. Major employers include Accenture, Genpact, and Cognizant.

**Online tutor.** If you speak English fluently and have a bachelor's degree in anything, you qualify. If you do not have a degree, some platforms accept current students or relevant experience. Companies like VIPKid, Preply, and Cambly pay $12 to $25 an hour. The schedule is flexible. The catch is time zones.

## Where to actually find these jobs

General job boards like Indeed and LinkedIn are crowded. Everyone applies there. The better strategy is to go where these specific roles get posted.

**FlexJobs** vets every listing for legitimacy. It costs $2.95 for a two-week trial, which is worth it when you are starting because it filters out scams. Their Q2 2026 report shows entry level remote listings up 34 percent year over year.

**We Work Remotely** has a dedicated customer support and administrative category. Volume is lower, but the signal-to-noise ratio is better than anywhere else.

**Upwork** is the fastest path to your first paid remote work. Set your rate low for the first two or three projects. The goal is not money. It is a review that says "reliable, communicates well, delivered on time." That review is worth more than a resume.

**Company websites directly.** TTEC, Concentrix, LiveOps, Williams-Sonoma, and American Express all list remote customer service jobs on their own career pages. These postings get a fraction of the applicants that Indeed listings do.

## How to make your application work when you have no experience

A resume with zero remote work history does not have to be a liability. It just has to tell a different story.

**Lead with reliability, not credentials.** Remote managers care about one thing above everything else: will this person show up, do the work, and communicate clearly without someone looking over their shoulder. If you have ever held any job for more than a year, led a volunteer project, managed a family schedule, or ran a side hustle, you have evidence of reliability. Put that front and center.

**Get a remote-relevant certification.** Google's Project Management Certificate, HubSpot's Inbound Marketing Certification, or even a typing speed test result. These take anywhere from four hours to a few weeks and cost little or nothing. They signal that you are serious and self-directed. Two traits remote hiring managers value more than experience.

**Address the remote thing directly.** Include a sentence in your cover letter or profile that sounds like this: "I have set up a dedicated workspace with a backup internet connection and have experience managing my own schedule independently." Most entry level applicants skip this. The ones who do not, stand out.

**Do a test project.** For VA and freelance roles, offer to do a small paid trial. A single afternoon of calendar organization or inbox cleanup. Nobody says no to a risk-free test drive.

---

## Three things to avoid

**Jobs that ask you to pay for training.** Legitimate companies do not charge you to start working. If a listing asks for a training fee, equipment deposit, or "registration payment," close the tab.

**Listings with vague job descriptions.** "Make money from home, flexible hours, unlimited earning potential" means commission-only sales. That is not a job. It is a lottery.

**Applying to a hundred jobs at once.** A targeted application that references the company by name and addresses the specific role beats fifty copy-paste applications. The entry level remote job market rewards effort, not volume.

---

Getting your first remote job without experience is mostly about aiming at the right target. Customer service, virtual assistant work, data entry, tutoring, content moderation. These are not glamorous. They will not make you rich. But they get you in the door. Six months of reliable remote work on your resume, and the next job hunt looks completely different.
