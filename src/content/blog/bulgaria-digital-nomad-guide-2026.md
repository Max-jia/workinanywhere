Bulgaria is the cheapest country in the European Union for digital nomads. Its capital, Sofia, offers a quality of life comparable to Central European cities at roughly half the cost. It runs a flat 10 percent income tax. It is a Schengen member as of 2024. And most digital nomads have never considered it.

I spent two weeks in Sofia in 2025, scouting it as a potential base for the winter months. I went in skeptical. I left convinced that Bulgaria is the most underrated nomad destination in Europe.

---

## Visas and Residency: Straightforward Paths

Bulgaria does not have a dedicated digital nomad visa, but it has several workable paths for remote workers:

**Type D Long-Stay Visa (Freelancer Route):** This is the main path for digital nomads. You register as a freelancer in Bulgaria, apply for a Type D visa at a Bulgarian consulate, and once approved, you receive a residence permit. The process takes two to four months and requires proof of income, accommodation, and health insurance. Once granted, the permit is valid for up to one year and renewable.

**Schengen Tourist Stay (Short-Term Route):** Bulgaria joined the Schengen Area in 2024. Most Western passport holders can stay visa-free for up to 90 days within any 180-day period. This is the simplest option for short stays and does not require registration or tax obligations.

**The Tax Advantage:** Bulgaria has a flat 10 percent personal income tax rate, one of the lowest in the EU. Combined with social security contributions (around 14 percent for freelancers), the total tax burden is roughly 24 percent, compared to 35-50 percent in most Western European countries. For a freelancer earning $60,000 a year, the difference amounts to $10,000 or more annually.

A British freelance writer who moved to Sofia in 2024 told me: "I was paying 40 percent tax in the UK. In Bulgaria, I pay 10 percent plus social contributions. My accountant costs me €50 a month. The entire setup took six weeks."

## Cost of Living: The Numbers

Bulgaria is genuinely cheap, and not just by European standards. It rivals Southeast Asia on cost while offering EU-quality infrastructure, healthcare, and legal protections.

## Best Cities for Digital Nomads

### Sofia: The Capital and Hub

Sofia is where most nomads start. It has the most coworking spaces, the largest expat community, and the best infrastructure in the country. The city is a mix of Soviet-era architecture, Roman ruins, modern glass office towers, and a mountain that rises directly from the southern edge of the city (Vitosha Mountain, accessible by bus in 30 minutes).

The coworking scene is concentrated around the city center and the Lozenets neighborhood. Betahaus and Puzl are the two largest spaces. Both offer flexible day passes and monthly memberships, and both run regular events that are genuinely good for meeting people.

The downside: traffic is terrible, the air quality worsens in winter when people burn wood for heating, and the city is not a beauty. It grows on you, but it does not impress on first sight.

**Best for:** Long-term nomads who want a full-service city, fast internet, a growing community, and the lowest cost of living in any EU capital.

### Bansko: The Nomad Mountain Town

Bansko is a ski town at the foot of the Pirin Mountains that has evolved into one of Europe's most concentrated digital nomad communities. In winter, it is a budget ski destination. In summer, it is a hiking and coworking hub. Year-round, it attracts remote workers drawn by the extremely low cost of living and the tight community.

Bansko Nomad Fest, held each June, brings together hundreds of remote workers for a week of talks, workshops, and networking. Coworking Bansko was one of the first dedicated nomad coworking spaces in Europe and remains the anchor of the community.

A developer from Canada who spent eight months in Bansko told me: "You can wake up, ski for two hours, be at your desk by 10am, and spend $12 on food for the entire day. The community is small enough that you actually know people. It is not anonymous the way Chiang Mai has become."

**Best for:** Nomads who want a small, tight community, access to mountains, and the lowest possible cost of living in the EU.

### Plovdiv: Culture and Calm

Plovdiv is Bulgaria's second city and one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in Europe. The old town is a maze of cobblestone streets, Roman ruins, and restored 19th-century houses. The modern city is significantly cheaper than Sofia and much less touristy than Bansko.

The expat community is smaller but welcoming. Coworking options exist but are limited. Most remote workers here use cafes and home setups. The pace of life is slower than Sofia. If you want culture, calm, and a more authentic Bulgarian experience, Plovdiv delivers.

**Best for:** Nomads who want culture over convenience, history lovers, people who do not need a large expat community.

## Internet and Infrastructure

Bulgaria consistently ranks among the top countries in the world for internet speed. According to Speedtest Global Index data, Bulgarian fixed broadband ranks among the top 20 globally, ahead of the UK, Germany, and most of Western Europe. A 100 Mbps fiber connection costs €10 to €15 a month.

Mobile data is equally cheap. An unlimited 5G plan costs €10 to €15 a month. Coverage is good in cities and along major transport corridors, weaker in rural mountain areas.

Public transit in Sofia is functional but unremarkable. The metro covers the main parts of the city. Buses fill the gaps. Taxis are cheap (around €0.50 per km) but use the TaxiMe or Yellow app rather than hailing on the street to avoid overcharging.

## What Nobody Tells You

The customer service culture is direct to the point of rudeness by Western standards. A waiter will not smile at you. A government clerk will not make small talk. This is not hostility. It is just the default interaction style, and it takes some getting used to.

The Cyrillic alphabet is a barrier in daily life. Street signs, menus, and product labels are in Bulgarian. Google Translate's camera mode handles menus easily, but navigating administrative tasks takes patience and sometimes a Bulgarian-speaking friend.

Winter in Sofia means poor air quality from wood burning and old diesel cars. If you have respiratory issues, plan to be out of the city between December and February. Bansko and the mountain towns have much cleaner air in the same season.

## FAQ

**Can I stay in Bulgaria long-term as a freelancer?**
Yes. The Type D visa and freelancer registration path allows stays of up to one year, renewable. After five years of continuous legal residency, you can apply for permanent residency. After 10 years, EU long-term residency.

**Is Bulgaria safe?**
Yes. Violent crime is rare. Petty theft and pickpocketing occur in tourist areas. Use standard urban precautions. Sofia feels safe walking alone at night in most neighborhoods.

**Do I need to speak Bulgarian?**
In Sofia and the major cities, younger people speak English. In smaller towns and when dealing with older officials, English coverage drops significantly. Google Translate handles everyday situations. For administrative tasks, having a Bulgarian-speaking friend or fixer saves hours of frustration.
