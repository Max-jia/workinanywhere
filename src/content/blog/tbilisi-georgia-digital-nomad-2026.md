Tbilisi sits at the crossroads of Europe and Asia, and in 2026 it's becoming one of the most talked-about digital nomad destinations in the world — without the crowds and prices of Lisbon or Bali.

Here's what makes Georgia's capital unique, and why remote workers are increasingly choosing it as a long-term base.

---

## The Visa: 365 Days, No Questions

Citizens of 95+ countries — including the US, UK, EU, Canada, and Australia — can stay in Georgia for **up to 365 days visa-free**. No application. No fee. No income proof. You show up at the border with a valid passport, and you're in for a year.

There is also a formal "Remotely from Georgia" program with no minimum income requirement — just proof of remote work. As of March 2026, new rules require foreign freelancers conducting paid activities to register for a work permit and residence permit, so check current requirements before arriving.

Travel medical insurance with at least 30,000 GEL (~$10,800) coverage is mandatory from January 2026.

## The Tax System (The Real Draw)

Georgia has one of the lowest tax burdens in the world for small business owners and freelancers:

| Tax Status | Rate | Turnover Cap |
|------------|------|-------------|
| Micro Business | **0%** | 30,000 GEL (~$11,000)/year |
| Small Business Status | **1%** | 500,000 GEL (~$180,000)/year |
| Standard IE | 20% on net profit | No cap |
| IT Virtual Zone | 0% corporate tax, 5% on dividends | IT export income only |

Most freelancers register for Small Business Status: 1% tax on gross revenue up to ~$180,000/year. This is genuinely one of the most favorable tax environments for remote workers anywhere.

Registration is straightforward: visit a Public Service Hall or apply online → get a Tax ID → register as an Individual Entrepreneur → apply for SBS → open a bank account (TBC or Bank of Georgia).

**Tax residency triggers at 183+ days.** Once you pass this threshold, you file and pay in Georgia — but at the rates above, many nomads consider this a feature, not a bug.

## Coworking & Community

Tbilisi has 30+ coworking spaces with 90–150 Mbps fiber standard. Standouts include:

- **Fabrika Coworking** — The social hub. Located in a repurposed Soviet sewing factory compound with a courtyard, street food vendors, bars, and a hostel. Best for meeting other nomads.
- **Impact Hub Tbilisi** — Professional, structured environment. Meeting rooms and fixed desks. Better for quiet focus and client calls.
- **Terminal** — Popular space in a former printing house with a strong community vibe.
- **Stamba** — Part of the Stamba Hotel complex; stylish, creative atmosphere.

## Food & Wine

Georgia invented wine 8,000 years ago. A glass of natural qvevri wine costs $2–4. The food is outstanding and absurdly affordable:

- Khachapuri (cheese-filled bread boat): ~$3
- Khinkali (soup dumplings): ~$0.70 each
- Full dinner with wine: $10–15

## What to Know

**Pros:**
- 1-year visa-free entry for most Western passports
- Extremely favorable tax environment (1% for most freelancers)
- Affordable cost of living with European-city infrastructure
- Incredible food and wine culture
- Growing, welcoming nomad community

**Cons:**
- New 2026 work permit requirements add some bureaucracy
- English is not widely spoken outside the nomad/city center bubble
- Infrastructure (sidewalks, power) can be inconsistent
- Winter is cold (not extreme, but not Mediterranean)
- Direct flights limited compared to major European hubs

---

**Bottom line:** Tbilisi offers one of the best combinations of affordability, visa freedom, and tax efficiency anywhere in the world for remote workers in 2026. The nomad community here is smaller than in Chiang Mai or Lisbon — which, depending on what you're looking for, may be exactly the point.
