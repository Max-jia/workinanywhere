Mexico City is hosting 13 World Cup matches. Not the most of any host nation — the US has 78 — but pound for pound, CDMX might be the smartest place to park your laptop during this tournament.

Here's why: $3 beers. $12 meals. Taxis at 46 cents per kilometer. A daily budget of $45-65 that leaves you room to actually enjoy the World Cup without checking your bank account between every round.

But there are things you need to know before you show up. The internet situation is not what you're used to. The stadium is not where you want to stay. And the city is massive enough that picking the wrong neighborhood means spending more time in traffic than at your desk or at a match.

---

## Where to Actually Stay

Do not stay near Estadio Azteca. It's in the south of the city, far from the coworking and nomad hubs. You'll be isolated, overpaying for match-proximity accommodation, and spending hours in traffic to get anywhere worth being.

Stay in Roma Norte or Condesa. These are the neighborhoods where digital nomads already live. 171 coworking spaces exist across the city, and the highest concentration is here. You'll find cafes with reliable WiFi, people who speak English, and a community that's been hosting remote workers for years. The matches are a Metro ride away. Your workday doesn't have to be.

Juárez, just north of Roma, is 40% cheaper and walking distance to the same cafes. If you're budget-conscious, this is your play.

---

## The Internet: Plan Ahead

Let's be direct about this: Mexico City's average broadband speed is 43.6 Mbps. That's fine. It's not great. It'll handle email, documents, and standard-definition video calls. It will struggle with HD streaming, large file transfers, or anything bandwidth-heavy while the rest of the building is also online.

Here's what works:
- **Book coworking spaces with verified speeds.** Don't rely on cafe WiFi descriptions. Spaces in Roma Norte and Condesa typically have dedicated fiber connections that outperform residential broadband.
- **Bring a backup eSIM.** When the WiFi fails — and during the World Cup, with thousands of extra people in the city, it will fail sometimes — switch to mobile data. Airalo and Holafly both have solid Mexico plans.
- **Use a VPN.** Mexico's cybersecurity scores are below the 80-point benchmark considered "good." A VPN is infrastructure, not a luxury, especially if you're handling client work or accessing financial accounts.

## The Visa Situation

This is the best part: 180-day tourist visa on arrival for most Western passport holders. No application. No income proof. No waiting. You land, you're in for six months. It's the lowest-friction entry of any World Cup host city.

---

## What to Know Before You Go

The altitude is 7,350 feet. You will feel it. Headaches, fatigue, shortness of breath — give yourself 3-5 days to adjust before judging the city. Drink twice as much water as you think you need. Go easy on the mezcal the first week.

Spanish is not optional here. You can get by in Roma and Condesa without it, but your quality of life improves dramatically with even basic Spanish. Learn how to order food, ask for directions, and say thank you. It matters.

## The Bottom Line

Mexico City during the World Cup is not a polished, frictionless experience. The internet requires planning. The altitude takes adjustment. The city is chaotic in ways that will either charm you or exhaust you.

But if you want to work remotely during this tournament without spending a fortune, there is no better option. The city is alive. The food is world-class. The people are welcoming. And at $45-65 a day, you can actually afford to enjoy it.

## FAQ

**Is Mexico City safe during the World Cup?**
Yes, especially in Roma Norte, Condesa, Juarez, and Polanco. These neighborhoods are busy, well-policed, and full of locals and visitors. Use Uber at night, avoid isolated streets, and follow the same common-sense precautions you would in any major city. The World Cup brings more crowds and more police presence.

**Can I work remotely from Mexico City during the tournament?**
Yes. Internet in Roma and Condesa is reliable (50-100 Mbps). Coworking spaces are plentiful. The main challenges are time zone alignment with your team and the altitude adjustment in the first few days. Mexico City is in the Central Time Zone, which aligns with US working hours.

**Where should I stay in Mexico City as a remote worker?**
Roma Norte and Condesa are the best neighborhoods for cafes, coworking, and walkability. Juarez is slightly cheaper and next door. See our [full Mexico City neighborhood guide](/blog/mexico-city-neighborhood-guide-2026) for rent prices and local vibe by area.
