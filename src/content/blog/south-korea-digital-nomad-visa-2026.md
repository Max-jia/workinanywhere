South Korea just made a quiet move that changes the nomad visa landscape. On June 30, the country officially launched its F-1-D Workation Visa, converting a two-year pilot into permanent law. The big news is not that Korea has a nomad visa. It is how they structured it.

The income threshold now depends on your age and where you live. If you are between 18 and 34 and willing to live outside the Seoul metro area, you need roughly $37,000 a year. If you are 35 or older and want to live in Seoul, you need about $74,000. Same visa. Different math.

Most nomad visas use a single income number. Portugal requires €3,680 a month whether you are 25 or 55. Spain requires roughly €2,850. Korea decided that a 28-year-old freelancer living in Busan should not have to prove the same income as a 45-year-old executive in Gangnam.

This makes Korea's visa genuinely more accessible to younger nomads than most European alternatives. The trade-off is geographic. Seoul is one of the world's great cities, and the higher threshold to live there reflects that. If you are willing to base yourself in Busan, Daegu, or Gwangju, the financial barrier drops substantially.

---

## What You Actually Need

The visa allows up to three years of stay, renewed annually. You cannot work for a Korean employer. Your income must come from a foreign company or your own foreign business, and you need at least one year of employment history with that company.

You need a clean criminal background check with an Apostille. You need private health insurance covering at least 100 million won, roughly $70,000-75,000 in medical and repatriation coverage. Your spouse and minor children can join you under your application. The visa fee is about $45 for US applicants.

The income tiers break down like this. Ages 18-34, living outside Greater Seoul: roughly $37,000 per year. Ages 35 and older, living in Seoul, Incheon, or Gyeonggi Province: roughly $74,000. Everyone else falls into the middle tier at about $55,000.

During the pilot program, every applicant regardless of age or location needed the full 2x GNI, roughly $74,000. The new structure cuts that by half for younger nomads outside the capital. That is a deliberate policy choice to spread nomad spending beyond Seoul.

---

## Why This Matters

Korea was already on the nomad radar after the pilot program launched in 2024. Excellent internet. World-class public transit. Safe cities that function reliably at all hours. A food scene that rewards exploration. A culture that takes work seriously without being oppressive about it.

The permanent launch with tiered income requirements changes the math. A 30-year-old developer earning $50,000 from a remote US job could not qualify for the pilot at the old $74,000 threshold. Now they qualify for the middle tier. A 26-year-old freelancer earning $40,000 could not even come close before. Now they can if they live outside Seoul.

This is not the cheapest nomad destination in Asia. It is not Thailand or Vietnam. But for someone who wants Asian infrastructure without the chaos, who values safety and efficiency, and who can meet the income requirements, Korea just opened a door that was previously closed to a lot of people.
