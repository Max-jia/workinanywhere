Every guide tells you Chiang Mai is the perfect nomad city. Cheap rent. Great food. Friendly community. What they skip is the part between landing at CNX and actually feeling settled. That first week is disorienting. It is also when you make the decisions that determine whether you thrive here or leave after a month.

I have watched too many people arrive, book a year-long lease from a Facebook listing they never visited, and end up in a neighborhood they hate. Do the opposite.

---

## Day 1: Land. Do Not Make Decisions.

You just crossed eight time zones. You are tired. Everything is new. You will want to "be productive" and start apartment hunting immediately. Do not do this. You will make bad choices.

Get a SIM card at the airport. AIS or True, 200-600 THB for a month of data. You need your passport. The kiosk staff will set it up for you.

Withdraw cash. 5,000-10,000 THB in small bills. Street food stalls, songthaews, and markets take cash only. The ATM will offer to charge you in your home currency. Decline. Always choose local currency. Dynamic currency conversion adds 3-5% to your withdrawal for no reason.

Install Grab. It is the Uber of Thailand. You will use it until you learn the songthaew system and probably for weeks after that.

Go to your short-term accommodation. You booked 4-6 nights, not a three-month lease. Good. Eat something. Sleep. The city will still be here tomorrow.

---

## Days 2-3: Walk the Sois

Rentals here work differently from what you are used to. The best apartments never appear online. Landlords put yellow or red "For Rent" signs on their gates and wait for someone to knock. The apartments on Airbnb and Facebook groups are the leftovers that locals did not want.

Walk the sois in Nimmanhaemin. Walk the sois in Santitham. When you see a sign, take a photo of the phone number and call or message. Most landlords speak enough English to arrange a viewing. If they do not, send a message in English. They will use Google Translate. It works.

View at least five apartments before you commit. Test the water pressure. Run a WiFi speed test inside the unit, not in the lobby. Visit the street at different times of day. A quiet soi at 11am can be a karaoke bar at 10pm.

Do not rent a scooter yet. You do not know the traffic flow. You do not know which intersections are dangerous. Wait until week two.

---

## Days 4-5: Negotiate Like a Local

Revisit your top two or three apartments. View them again. Then negotiate.

Landlords here expect you to haggle. If you are standing in front of them with cash and a passport, you have leverage. "I can sign today. What is your real price?" will typically get you 15-25% off the listed rent.

DTV visa holders get better deals. Landlords know you are staying 6-12 months. Mention it. Long-term tenants are rare and valuable.

The contract will be in Thai. Use Google Translate. The key terms are rent amount, deposit (usually one month), notice period, and whether utilities are included. Get these confirmed in writing, even if it is just a WhatsApp message in English.

---

## Day 6: Set Up Your Infrastructure

Open a Bangkok Bank or Kasikorn account. You need your passport and a residence certificate from Immigration. Your landlord files a TM30 form within 24 hours of your arrival. Ask them to do this on day one.

Tour the coworking spaces. Punspace, MANA, Hub53, Yellow. Buy a day pass at two or three before committing to a monthly membership. The vibe matters more than the amenities.

Find your local: the coffee shop you will return to every morning, the street food stall where the owner recognizes you, the market where you buy fruit. Familiarity is grounding when everything else is new.

---

## Day 7: Eat Khao Soi

Not pad thai. Khao soi. It is the dish of northern Thailand, a coconut curry noodle soup with crispy noodles on top. Go to Khao Soi Khun Yai near the Old City. Arrive before they sell out, usually by 1pm. This is not just a meal. It is a ritual that marks the end of your first week and the beginning of actually living here.

## What Everything Costs in Week One

Numbers help. Here is what a realistic first week in Chiang Mai costs, based on mid-2026 prices:

## FAQ

**Do I need a visa for Chiang Mai?**
Most Western passport holders get 60 days visa-free on arrival, extendable by 30 days at the immigration office for 1,900 THB ($54). The Destination Thailand Visa (DTV) allows stays up to 180 days for remote workers and is the best long-term option. For the full picture, see our [guide to 50+ nomad visas](/blog/50-countries-nomad-visas-2026).

**What is the best neighborhood for digital nomads?**
Nimmanhaemin is the default. Coworking spaces, cafes, and the largest expat concentration. Santitham is cheaper, quieter, and more local. The Old City is charming but more touristy. Try Nimman first. If you want more local flavor, walk Santitham on day two.

**Do I need to speak Thai?**
No. You can live comfortably in Chiang Mai with English. Learn hello (sawadee khrap/kha), thank you (khop khun khrap/kha), and numbers 1-10. That gets you 90 percent of the way. Anything beyond that earns you genuine goodwill.
