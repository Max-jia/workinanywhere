If you are a digital nomad in Bali working on a tourist visa, you need to stop. Not slow down. Not be more careful. Stop.

In April, Indonesian immigration created the Dharma Dewata Task Force. It is roughly 100 officers whose only job is to find foreigners working illegally on tourist visas. They patrol Canggu, Ubud, Seminyak, and Uluwatu. They monitor social media in real time. They review sponsored posts, tagged brand partnerships, and barter deals as evidence.

Since January, 165 people have been deported. Another 62 have been detained. The fines reach 500 million Indonesian rupiah, roughly $30,000. You can be banned from the country for life.

---

## What Counts as Work

The Indonesian government has been explicit. Work includes any activity that generates promotional or economic value, even without cash payment. A hotel gives you a free stay in exchange for Instagram posts. That is work. A restaurant comps your meal for a TikTok. That is work. You shoot portfolio photos for a brand that pays you later. That is work.

The government's position, quoted in multiple enforcement statements: it is not about whether money changes hands. If a business gains promotion and you receive a benefit, you are working. The tourist visa covers tourism. It does not cover content creation, brand deals, barter arrangements, or remote work performed from a cafe while you post about it on social media.

The task force is not just checking paperwork at immigration. They are scrolling Instagram. They are watching TikTok. They are looking at the geotags on your posts and cross-referencing them with your visa status. The evidence they need to detain you is already on your profile.

---

## What Visa You Actually Need

The E33G Remote Worker Visa is the correct option for digital nomads. It requires $60,000 a year in income and a $2,000 minimum bank balance. It is valid for one year and renewable. You cannot work for Indonesian companies or earn income in Indonesia.

There is also a C5A Content Creator Visa designed for influencers filming monetized content. It exists in law but is not yet integrated into the immigration portal, which means applying for it is currently impractical.

If you do not meet the income threshold for the E33G, you have two choices. Come to Bali as a tourist and do not work. No posts. No brand deals. No remote work, even if your clients are overseas. Or go somewhere with a lower visa barrier. Thailand, Vietnam, and Malaysia all have more accessible options.

---

## This Is Not Going to Relax

Bali's government has been clear about its direction. They want "quality tourism." They want visitors who spend money without straining infrastructure, following local laws, and leaving quietly. The influencer economy that grew around Canggu and Ubud over the past decade is explicitly not part of that vision.

The enforcement will get stricter, not looser. The task force is new and expanding. The monitoring tools are improving. The penalties are publicized intentionally to deter others. If you are in Bali right now on a tourist visa and earning income in any form, you should either get the correct visa or leave before someone else makes that decision for you.
