While Southeast Asian countries fight over digital nomad visa requirements, Canada quietly built something simpler: a policy that lets remote workers with foreign employers stay for up to six months without a visa, with a path to convert to a work permit if they want to stay longer.

Implemented in 2023, Canada's digital nomad roadmap hasn't gotten the attention it deserves. The World Cup is changing that. Toronto and Vancouver are hosting matches, and nomads arriving for the tournament are discovering a policy that makes long-term stays surprisingly straightforward.

---

## What the Policy Actually Says

Here's the core: if you work remotely for a foreign employer, you can enter Canada as a visitor and stay for up to six months. No visa application. No income threshold. No proof of savings. You just need to demonstrate that your work is for a company outside Canada and that you can support yourself during your stay.

The key distinction: you're entering as a visitor who happens to work remotely. You're not entering on a "digital nomad visa" — Canada doesn't have one. This matters because it means you're governed by visitor rules, not work permit rules. You cannot work for Canadian companies or Canadian clients. Your income must come from outside the country.

The six months can potentially be converted into a work permit if you receive a job offer from a Canadian employer during your stay. This is the "roadmap" part: come as a visitor, experience the country, and if you find an opportunity to contribute to the Canadian economy, there's a path to stay.

## Why It Matters Right Now

Toronto scored 200 out of 250 points in the World Cup remote work rankings — the highest of any host city. Vancouver was second at 163. The infrastructure is genuine: 200+ Mbps broadband, dense coworking networks, strong cybersecurity, and time zones that overlap with European working hours.

During the World Cup, Toronto alone expects $3.8 billion in economic impact. Digital nomads are contributing to that — not just through tourism spending, but through extended stays that involve coworking memberships, apartment rentals, and integration into local business communities.

---

## Toronto vs. Vancouver

Both cities work under the same national policy. The experience is different.

**Toronto** is the business hub. More coworking spaces (113), better transit, more diverse food scene, more expensive accommodation. If you want to be in the center of things — especially during the World Cup — Toronto delivers.

**Vancouver** is the lifestyle pick. Better scenery, more outdoor access, slightly better internet speeds, slightly worse time zone alignment with Europe. If you value nature and a slower pace, Vancouver wins.

Both are expensive by global nomad standards. Budget $110-140 CAD/day in either city. This is not a budget destination. It's a quality-of-life destination for people who can afford it.

## What to Know Before You Go

**Banking:** Open a Canadian bank account if you're staying more than a couple of months. Wise and Revolut work but local banking makes life easier for rent payments and daily transactions.

**Healthcare:** Canada's healthcare system does not cover visitors. You need travel health insurance. SafetyWing, World Nomads, and Genki all cover Canada. Do not skip this — out-of-pocket medical costs in Canada are significant.

**Accommodation:** Airbnb works for short stays. For anything over a month, use local rental platforms or negotiate directly with landlords. Furnished Finder (originally built for traveling nurses) has expanded into Canada and is worth checking.

**Coworking:** Book ahead, especially in Toronto. World Cup demand is filling spaces. Day passes run $30-40 CAD. Monthly memberships are better value if you're staying 3+ weeks.

---

## Is Canada Right for You?

Canada makes sense if you want North American infrastructure with a more welcoming policy environment than the US, and you can afford the cost of living. It doesn't make sense if you're budget-constrained or looking for a tropical beach lifestyle.

The six-month policy is genuinely generous. The work permit pathway is genuinely useful. And if you're already planning to be in North America for the World Cup, there's no better time to take advantage of it.
