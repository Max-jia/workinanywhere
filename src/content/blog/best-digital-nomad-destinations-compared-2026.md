The hardest part of starting as a digital nomad is not the job or the money. It is picking where to go first. There are more than 50 countries with digital nomad visas now. The choice is paralyzing.

I have asked nomads in a dozen cities what they actually spend, how reliable the internet is, and whether they would do it again. Here is what holds up across the board.

## Chiang Mai, Thailand — the first-timer's default for a reason

If there is a place designed to make your first month as a nomad easy, it is Chiang Mai. Nimmanhaemin Road alone has more coworking spaces per block than most countries have total. The local economy runs on people like you showing up with a laptop and staying a while.

You can land, find a furnished apartment in a day, join a coworking space in an afternoon, and have a social circle by the end of the week. The food costs less than cooking would back home. The internet is fast enough for video calls. The weather is warm year-round, with a smoky season from February to April that you should plan around.

It is not the cheapest city in Southeast Asia anymore. It is still the easiest.

---

## Medellín, Colombia — where Google Trends is pointing hardest

Medellín has spent the last decade rewriting its reputation, and the digital nomad community has noticed. Colombia now tops Google search rankings for nomad destinations heading into 2026. The new digital nomad visa allows a two-year stay, longer than almost anywhere else.

The city delivers what nomads actually need. Modern apartments in Laureles and El Poblado at $500 to $800 a month. Reliable internet. Coworking spaces with good coffee. A springlike climate that does not change much. The metro system works. The Spanish is clear enough to learn fast.

Safety is the question everyone asks. The honest answer: most nomads have zero issues. Stay in the established neighborhoods. Do not flash expensive things. Take Ubers at night. The same rules that apply in any large Latin American city will keep you fine here.

---

## Da Nang, Vietnam — the value play that keeps getting better

Da Nang does not get the attention that Chiang Mai or Bali get, and that is part of the appeal. The beach is clean and long. The seafood is cheap and fresh. A furnished studio within walking distance of a coworking space runs $300 to $450 a month.

The internet held steady at 40 to 100 Mbps across most spaces. The visa situation is less generous than Colombia or Georgia. Most nomads do monthly border runs or a three-month tourist visa. It is a minor hassle, not a dealbreaker.

If you want a beach town where you can work productively and spend under $900 a month total, Da Nang is hard to beat.

---

## Lisbon, Portugal — Europe's entry point, getting pricier

Lisbon became the default European nomad hub between 2021 and 2024, and the rent shows it. A one-bedroom in a central neighborhood now costs $1,000 to $1,500 a month. That is still cheaper than most Western European capitals. It is not the bargain it was three years ago.

What Lisbon still offers is quality of life. The light is beautiful. The food is excellent. The city is walkable in a way few capitals are. The nomad community is large and easy to plug into. Coworking spaces are everywhere. The D8 visa opens a path to long-term residency.

If you earn in dollars or euros, Lisbon is comfortable. If you earn less and need to stretch every dollar, look at Porto or Braga instead.

---

## Mexico City — the one that surprises people

Mexico City does not immediately register as a digital nomad hub, but the community here is growing faster than most people realize. Roma Norte and Condesa feel like neighborhoods purpose-built for people who work from cafes. The food scene alone is worth the trip.

Rent runs $600 to $1,000 for a nice one-bedroom in the expat-heavy areas. The internet is reliable in those neighborhoods. The time zone lines up with US working hours, which matters if you have American clients or colleagues.

The visa is straightforward. Most people enter on a 180-day tourist permit and figure out longer-term options from there.

---

## Tokyo, Japan — the infrastructure play

The weak yen has made Tokyo accessible in a way it has not been for decades. A decent apartment in a central neighborhood costs $700 to $1,200 a month. Groceries and eating out are cheaper than most US cities. The internet is among the fastest in the world.

The catch has always been the visa, and that is finally changing. Japan's new digital nomad visa launched in early 2026. It is not as generous as Colombia or Georgia, but it opens a door that was previously bolted shut.

Tokyo is the right pick if infrastructure and safety are your top priorities and you earn enough to cover the higher base costs.

---

## The trend that matters in 2026

The nomad visa race is real. Countries are competing for remote workers, which means better terms every year. Georgia offers one-year visa-free entry and a one percent small business tax. Colombia gives two-year stays. South Korea's new visa has age-tiered income requirements. More than 50 countries now have a dedicated digital nomad visa.

The practical takeaway: you have more options than ever, and the competition between countries is making the terms better every year. Pick based on what your first three months actually need to look like. Easy visa. Reliable internet. A community that will actually talk to you. Everything else is negotiable.
