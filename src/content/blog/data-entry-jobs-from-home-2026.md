When I first started looking at remote work options, I assumed data entry would be the easiest way in. Type some things into a spreadsheet, get paid. How hard could it be?

The answer, it turns out, is that the actual work is not hard. What is hard is finding a real job amid the flood of scams. Of the roughly two dozen entry-level data entry listings I checked across major job boards one afternoon in July 2026, about a third were fake. Another third were real but paid below minimum wage. The remaining third were legitimate jobs that received hundreds of applications within hours of posting.

This article is what I wish I had read before starting. No promises of $60 per hour for mindless typing. Just which companies actually hire, what they pay, and the steps to take if you want one of these roles.

## What Data Entry Jobs Actually Pay in 2026

If a job posting offers $40 to $60 per hour for simple data entry with no experience, close the tab. It is a scam.

Real data entry pay in 2026:

| Type of Data Entry | Hourly Rate | Full-Time Annual |
|--------------------|-------------|------------------|
| General data entry | $15 to $22 | $32,000 to $45,000 |
| Medical records and coding | $20 to $28 | $42,000 to $58,000 |
| Legal document processing | $22 to $30 | $46,000 to $62,000 |
| Financial and invoice processing | $20 to $28 | $42,000 to $58,000 |
| Freelance/contract (entry level) | $14 to $22 | Varies by project |

Source: The Interview Guys 2026 remote data entry jobs analysis, verified FlexJobs and LinkedIn postings, July 2026.

This is not high-paying work. The rates are comparable to retail or food service. The difference is that you can do it from home, in your own clothes, on a schedule that may be flexible depending on the employer. For someone looking for a first remote job, someone re-entering the workforce, or someone who needs income that fits around caregiving responsibilities, that trade-off often makes sense.

## The Types of Data Entry Work Available

"Data entry" is a catch-all term that covers several different actual jobs. Understanding which one you are applying for matters.

**Pure data entry.** Typing information from one source into a database or spreadsheet. This is the simplest version and also the most competitive, because it requires the fewest skills. Most pure data entry roles are now automated or offshored. The ones that remain are often contract-based and pay on the lower end of the range.

**Data verification and quality assurance.** Checking existing data for errors, duplicates, or inconsistencies. This pays slightly better than pure entry because it requires more attention to detail and some familiarity with the systems being used.

**Medical and healthcare data.** Processing patient records, insurance claims, medical billing codes, and appointment data. Healthcare companies are the largest and most reliable source of remote data entry work. These roles often come with benefits if full-time. Medical coding certification (CPC through AAPC) bumps the pay to $25 to $35 per hour, but entry-level healthcare data roles do not require it.

**Legal document review and data processing.** Law firms and legal services companies hire remote data clerks to organize case files, enter court records, and process discovery documents. These jobs require confidentiality and sometimes a background check but rarely a degree.

**Financial data processing.** Accounts payable, accounts receivable, invoice processing, and expense report data entry. Staffing agencies like Robert Half place a high volume of these roles on a contract basis. They are often seasonal, tax season, year-end close, and insurance open enrollment periods create temporary surges in hiring.

**E-commerce product listing.** Entering product descriptions, prices, SKUs, and images into online store platforms like Shopify, Amazon, and WooCommerce. Small businesses and agencies hire for this on Upwork and Fiverr. These gigs pay $14 to $22 per hour and are genuine entry-level work.

## Which Companies Actually Hire

The safest way to find legitimate data entry work is to go directly to large companies that post these roles regularly. Here are the ones that come up consistently:

**UnitedHealth Group and Elevance Health.** Both appear on FlexJobs' 2026 Top 100 Remote Companies list. They hire for remote data entry, medical coding, claims processing, and billing roles. Standard corporate hiring process: apply through their career portal, interview with a real human, receive an offer letter. Entry-level healthcare data roles start at $15 to $20 per hour.

**CVS Health.** Similar to the above. Remote data roles in pharmacy records, patient data, and insurance processing.

**LexisNexis.** Hires remote data entry operators for legal and business data. Some roles require no prior experience. Standard benefits for full-time employees.

**Conduent.** A business process outsourcing company that handles data processing, customer service, and administrative support for large clients. Remote data roles are a core part of their business.

**Robert Half, Adecco, and Kelly Services.** These staffing agencies place contract and temp-to-hire data entry roles with companies that do not post those jobs publicly. Registering with all three takes about 30 minutes each and puts you in their database for future placements. Contract roles often have lower experience requirements and a faster hiring timeline than permanent positions.

**Boldly and BELAY.** Both hire part-time virtual assistants, which often includes data entry, calendar management, and administrative tasks. Pay is $18 to $28 per hour. You set your availability. These are real companies with real hiring processes.

## The Skills You Actually Need

Nobody asks for a degree for data entry work. The real requirements are:

**Typing speed.** Most postings ask for 40 to 50 words per minute. The candidates who get hired usually type 55 or higher. Test yourself at typingtest.com. If you are under 40 WPM, spend a week practicing before you apply. It is a faster return on time than sending applications that get filtered out at the skills screen.

**Accuracy.** 95% or better. Many employers use a typing test as part of the application process. Speed gets your application noticed. Accuracy gets you hired.

**Excel and Google Sheets.** Not just opening them. You need to know formulas (SUM, VLOOKUP, IF), pivot tables, data validation, and basic formatting. A weekend of YouTube tutorials gets you from zero to hireable on this front.

**Database familiarity.** Salesforce, QuickBooks, or industry-specific tools like Epic (healthcare) or Clio (legal). If you have never used any of them, mention on your resume that you are "proficient in database data entry and quick to learn new platforms." It is honest enough to pass a screening call, and most systems have similar basic interfaces.

## How to Avoid Scams

The Federal Trade Commission reports that "task scams" grew from nearly zero in 2020 to more than 48,000 reports in 2024. Data entry is the most impersonated category.

Five rules that filter out almost all of them:

1. **The pay test.** Real data entry pays $15 to $22 per hour. $40 or more is a scam. Nobody pays double the market rate for a job with no experience required.

2. **The communication test.** Legitimate companies do not recruit through WhatsApp, Telegram, Signal, or Instagram DMs. If someone contacts you on a chat app about a job, block and report.

3. **The money test.** Real employers never ask for money. Not for training, software, a background check, or a starter kit. Any upfront payment request is a scam.

4. **The process test.** Real jobs involve an interview with a human being who asks about your experience. If you get a job offer after filling out a single form or exchanging a few text messages, it is fake.

5. **The domain test.** Check the sender's email domain. If someone claiming to be from CVS Health emails from a Gmail address, it is a scam. Real recruiters use company domains. Watch for typo-squatted domains, too, "cvshelath.com" instead of "cvshealth.com" with one letter switched.

I once spent an afternoon tracing a data entry job listing on a major job board. The company name was real. The job description looked legitimate. But the email address had an extra letter in the domain. The "recruiter" asked for $49 for a "remote work certification." Three red flags that took five minutes to verify. The posting looked real enough that it had 200 applications.

## A Realistic Week-by-Week Plan

If you are starting from zero and want a remote data entry job, here is the sequence:

**Week 1: Build the basic skills.**
- Test your typing speed at typingtest.com. Practice daily until you hit 55 WPM with 95% accuracy.
- Watch YouTube tutorials on Excel formulas, pivot tables, and data validation. Build a practice spreadsheet to demonstrate you know how.
- Set up profiles on FlexJobs, LinkedIn, and the staffing agency sites (Robert Half, Adecco, Kelly Services).

**Week 2: Apply strategically.**
- Go to the career pages of UnitedHealth Group, Elevance Health, CVS Health, LexisNexis, and Conduent. Filter by remote and entry level. Apply to any data-related role that lists no degree requirement.
- On FlexJobs, search "data entry," "data verification," "records coordinator," "administrative assistant," and "virtual assistant." Filter by remote and entry level.
- On Upwork, create a profile that highlights your Excel skills and typing speed. Apply to small, fixed-price data entry projects to build reviews. Accept that the first few projects will pay poorly. They are resume builders, not income.

**Week 3: Follow up and expand.**
- If you have not heard back, register with two more staffing agencies.
- Set up a Google Alert for "remote data entry hiring 2026" to catch new company announcements.
- If you are open to related work, expand your search to customer service (Transcom, Concentrix) and virtual assistant roles (Boldly, BELAY, Time Etc).

**Week 4: Start applying to job boards.**
- Now that you have your skills and profiles set up, start applying to data entry postings on LinkedIn, Indeed, and ZipRecruiter, with the scam filters from the previous section running in your head.
- Most people do this in reverse, they spray applications at job boards first and build their skills later. By spending the first two weeks on skills and direct applications, you will have a better resume and less competition by the time you reach the general job boards.

## When Data Entry Makes Sense and When It Does Not

Data entry is the right first step if you need a remote job quickly, you do not have a degree or specialized training, and you can afford to earn $15 to $22 per hour while you build toward something else.

It is the wrong path if you have existing professional skills that transfer to higher-paying remote work. A former retail manager can get a remote customer service team lead role at $22 to $28 per hour. A former administrative assistant can get a remote executive assistant role at $25 to $35 per hour. Do not default to the lowest common denominator if you have experience that qualifies you for more.

I have seen people use data entry as a bridge: six to twelve months of remote income while they learn a higher-paying skill like bookkeeping, medical coding, or project management. That is where this path makes the most sense. Data entry is a starting point, not a career. Treat it as one.
