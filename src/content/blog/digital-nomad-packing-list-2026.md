Most first-time nomads pack too much. It's nearly universal. You imagine every scenario — the fancy dinner, the hiking trip, the client presentation, the rainy day — and pack for all of them. Then you carry that overstuffed bag up five flights of stairs in a building without an elevator in 35°C heat, and you immediately understand why experienced nomads travel light.

Here's a packing list built from the collective wisdom of nomad communities: what to bring, what to skip, and the things you only learn after your third repack.

---

## The Core Principle: Pack for 7-10 Days

If you can do laundry once a week (you can), you need 7-10 days of clothing. Not 30. Not "just in case." Seven to ten. You'll be amazed how little you actually need.

---

## Electronics — The Non-Negotiables

This is where weight and space are worth it. Your electronics are your livelihood.

### Essentials
- **Laptop** — The one decision that matters most. Prioritize battery life, portability, and a good keyboard over raw power unless your work demands it (video editing, 3D rendering).
- **Noise-canceling headphones** — Not a luxury. Infrastructure. For focus in cafes and coworking spaces, for clear calls, for blocking out construction noise and hostel common rooms. Over-ear (Sony WH-1000XM6, $449) or in-ear (AirPods Pro 2, $249; Sony WF-1000XM6, ~$300) depending on your preference.
- **Unlocked phone** — Dual SIM (physical + eSIM) is ideal. You'll swap local SIM cards in every new country.
- **Universal power adapter** — One with multiple USB-C and USB-A ports. Skip the single-country adapters.
- **Portable battery / power bank** — 20,000 mAh minimum. Charges your phone 3-4 times. Essential for long bus rides, airport delays, and cafes with no accessible outlets.
- **External hard drive or SSD for backups** — If your laptop dies or gets stolen, you need to keep working. Cloud backup (Backblaze, iCloud, Google Drive) plus a physical backup drive.

### Nice to Have (But Consider Weight)
- **Laptop stand** — The Roost V3 (~$89) folds flat. Raises your screen to eye level. Your neck and shoulders will notice the difference within a week.
- **Compact external keyboard and mouse** — Only if you work long hours. A folding Bluetooth keyboard weighs almost nothing.
- **Kindle or e-reader** — Lighter than books, battery lasts weeks. Better for reading than your phone or laptop, especially outdoors.
- **Small USB-C hub** — For connecting to external monitors, projectors, or wired internet when you need to.

### Skip
- **Tablet (unless it's your primary device)** — Redundant if you have a laptop and phone. Extra weight, extra thing to charge, extra thing to worry about.
- **Second monitor** — Too heavy, too fragile. Use the external monitor at your coworking space or accommodation instead.
- **Gaming console** — Too heavy. Cloud gaming services exist if you really need to game.

## Clothing — The 7-10 Day Rule

### The Formula
- 5-7 t-shirts / tops (merino wool if you can afford it — odor-resistant, dries fast, wears longer between washes)
- 2-3 button-downs or nicer tops (for video calls, client meetings, going out)
- 2-3 pairs of pants (one jeans, one lightweight, one versatile)
- 1-2 pairs of shorts
- 7-10 pairs of underwear and socks (merino wool socks are worth the investment)
- 1 lightweight jacket or hoodie (airplanes, air-conditioned cafes, cool evenings)
- 1 rain shell (if you're going anywhere with actual rain)
- 1 pair of versatile sneakers (walking, light hiking, casual wear)
- 1 pair of sandals or flip-flops
- 1 pair of "nice enough" shoes (if your lifestyle requires them — otherwise, skip)

### What Experienced Nomads Actually Wear
Stripes, neutrals, and solids. Everything matches everything else. If any shirt only goes with one specific pair of pants, don't bring it. You're optimizing for combinations, not outfits.

## Toiletries & Health

- Prescription medications for your full trip duration (plus copies of prescriptions)
- Basic first-aid kit: painkillers, antihistamines, antidiarrheal, band-aids, antiseptic cream
- Sunscreen and insect repellent (surprisingly expensive or hard to find in some regions)
- Minimal toiletries — you can buy shampoo, soap, toothpaste, and deodorant anywhere

---

## Documents (Digital + Physical Copies)

- Passport (with at least 6 months validity and plenty of blank pages)
- Printed copies of passport (stored separately from your actual passport)
- Digital copies of everything on encrypted cloud storage: passport, visas, insurance policy, vaccination records, driver's license, birth certificate
- Extra passport photos (some visas and applications require physical photos)
- Travel insurance card / policy number saved offline on your phone
- List of emergency contacts (including your country's embassy or consulate in each destination)

---

## The "You'll Thank Yourself Later" Items

These are small, cheap, and disproportionately useful:

- **Earplugs** (hostel dorm, thin apartment walls, barking dogs, roosters, construction)
- **Sleep mask** (jet lag recovery, inconsistent curtains, early sunrises)
- **Packing cubes** (the single best quality-of-life improvement for frequent travelers — keeps your bag organized, makes repacking trivial, lets you find things without emptying everything)
- **Combination lock** (hostel lockers, gym lockers, securing your bag in transit)
- **Reusable water bottle** (with a filter if you're going anywhere with questionable tap water)
- **Compact towel** (microfiber, quick-dry — many budget accommodations don't provide towels)
- **Laundry line or dry bag** (for hand-washing clothes in sinks — saves money, solves the "no laundry nearby" problem)

## What Experienced Nomads Wish They Hadn't Brought

From forum surveys and firsthand reports, the most common packing regrets:

1. **Too many shoes.** Shoes are heavy, bulky, and you'll wear the same one pair 90% of the time.
2. **"Just in case" formal wear.** Unless you have a specific event in your calendar (wedding, conference), you don't need a suit, dress shoes, or formal dress. If something comes up, you can buy or rent it locally.
3. **Books.** Switch to an e-reader or phone app. Books are dead weight.
4. **Full-size toiletries.** Buy them when you arrive. Travel-size for the first few days only.
5. **Too much "productivity gear."** You don't need a portable monitor, a mechanical keyboard, a vertical mouse, a laptop stand, and a separate webcam. Start with the essentials. Add only what you actually miss.
6. **Souvenirs and gifts from previous destinations.** Mail them home or don't buy them. Carrying gifts for people you might not see for months is unnecessary weight.

---

## One Final Rule

If you're not sure whether to bring something, don't. You can buy almost anything you need locally — and if you can't, you probably don't need it. Every item you don't pack is weight you don't carry up stairs, space you don't manage in a small rental, and one less thing to lose or break.

Pack for the life you actually live, not the life you imagine you might live.
