Every summer, the same "top digital nomad destinations" lists circulate. They're usually written by people who visited for a weekend two years ago and padded with SEO keywords.

This is different. Based on booking platform data, visa application volumes, coworking occupancy rates, and what nomad communities are actually talking about in mid-2026, here's where people are genuinely going.

---

## The World Cup Effect: North America Is Having a Moment

The World Cup is pulling nomads to North America in numbers the continent hasn't seen before. Toronto, Mexico City, and Houston are the top three destinations by booking volume. Nomads are arriving weeks before matches, settling into coworking spaces, and treating the tournament as an extended workcation.

Toronto leads for infrastructure. Mexico City leads for affordability. Houston leads for value — cheap transit, reasonable coworking, and an unexpected energy around the tournament that's drawing people who would normally be in Europe or Southeast Asia this time of year.

This is temporary. When the World Cup ends in late July, attention will shift back to the usual hubs. But for now, North America is genuinely where the action is.

---

## Southeast Asia: Still the Backbone

Chiang Mai, Da Nang, and Bali remain the top three Southeast Asian destinations by nomad population. Nothing has changed at the top.

What has changed is the distribution. Da Nang is growing faster than Chiang Mai — it's still smaller overall, but the growth rate suggests it's being discovered by nomads who find Chiang Mai too established or want a beach. Bali's enforcement of the E33G visa has made it slightly harder to access, but hasn't meaningfully reduced demand. People are just doing the paperwork now instead of hoping nobody checks.

---

## Eastern Europe: The Quiet Winner

Tbilisi, Georgia continues to attract nomads who want European proximity without European prices. One year visa-free for most Western passports. 1% small business tax. A cost of living that makes Western Europe look absurd. The community has grown from a few hundred nomads to several thousand in the past two years.

Bansko, Bulgaria has emerged as a secondary hub — a mountain town with surprisingly good coworking infrastructure and a winter sports scene that doubles as a social life. Budapest, Bucharest, and Sofia are all seeing increases in nomad arrivals, driven partly by rising costs in Lisbon and Barcelona pushing people east.

---

## What's Cooling Off

Lisbon's growth has plateaued. Rents have climbed 30-40% since 2020, and the city is actively debating how to manage tourism and expat pressure. It's still one of the best nomad cities in the world — just no longer a place you go to save money.

Bali's growth has been tempered by visa enforcement. The days of showing up on a tourist visa and hoping nobody asks questions are over. People who can meet the $60,000/year income threshold are still going. People who can't are looking elsewhere.

## What Smart Nomads Are Doing Differently This Summer

The ones who are thriving share three habits:

1. **They're staying longer in fewer places.** The 2-week city hop is fading. The 2-3 month base is becoming standard. People have realized that constantly moving is a recipe for burnout, not freedom.

2. **They're choosing cities based on visa policy first, Instagram second.** When a country makes it easy to stay legally and affordably, nomads respond. Georgia and Malaysia are benefiting from this. Indonesia's stricter enforcement is having the opposite effect.

3. **They're following events, not just weather.** The World Cup is the obvious example, but conferences, festivals, and community meetups are increasingly driving nomad movement patterns. People want to be where things are happening.
