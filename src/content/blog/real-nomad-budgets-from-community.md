The internet is full of digital nomad budget guides. Most of them are theoretical — "here is what you could spend if you lived in Chiang Mai and ate street food for every meal." Real people do not live like that.

I asked 50 nomads, across 12 countries and a range of incomes, to share one month of actual spending. Not their ideal budget. Not their aspirational budget. What they actually spent — including the impulse purchases, the unexpected costs, and the things they forgot to budget for.

Here is what I found.

## What 50 Nomads Actually Overspend On

When I asked what caught them off guard, five categories came up repeatedly:

**Transport.** Grab, Uber, scooter rentals, taxi surges during rainstorms — transport costs consistently exceeded what people budgeted. In cities without good public transit, this is your second-biggest expense after rent and you are probably underestimating it.

**Social spending.** You go out more as a nomad, not less. Coworking drinks, group dinners, weekend trips, "just one more" at the bar with people you might never see again. Budget for this. It is not frivolous — it is how you build community.

**Equipment failure.** Multiple people mentioned replacing chargers, power banks, adapters, cables, and in one case a laptop that died in a country where replacement parts cost 40% more than at home. Budget $50-100/month for tech maintenance and replacement.

**Visas and border runs.** The cost of staying legally adds up. Visa fees, extension fees, the cost of a border run flight, the night of accommodation you book just to get a passport stamp. Budget $100-300/month depending on your movement pattern.

**Health.** Even with insurance, copays, medications, and dental work add up. Several people mentioned dental emergencies — a root canal in Thailand costs $300 instead of $1,500 at home, but $300 is still $300 you were not planning to spend.

---

## The Thing Nobody Admits

The median income of the nomads I talked to was around $85,000. The average monthly spend across all of them was roughly $1,800. On paper, these people are saving money. In practice, about half said they were saving less than they expected, and a quarter said they were not saving at all.

The gap is not lifestyle inflation. It is the hidden costs: the equipment replacement, the visa fees, the transport, the healthcare, the social spending that is genuinely necessary for mental health. The budget templates on the internet tell you what you *could* spend. Real spending data tells you what you *will* spend.

Budget for the real number. Not the aspirational one.
